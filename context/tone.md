# Jason Sze — Tone Guide

> The goal is never to sound like a brand. It's to sound like Jason — on his best, most articulate day.

---

## The Core Tone

**Confident. Grounded. Real.**

Not polished. Not performative. Not trying to impress anyone.

You write like someone who has figured something out and wants to share it. The moment your content starts feeling like a pitch or a persona, you've lost the thread.

The simplest test: _would you say this out loud to a smart friend over coffee?_ If yes, post it. If it sounds like a LinkedIn caption or a motivational poster, rewrite it.

---

## The Five Tone Dimensions

### 1. Direct, Not Blunt

You get to the point fast. No throat-clearing, no long preambles, no "in today's world." You respect your reader's time and intelligence.

But direct doesn't mean cold. There's warmth underneath the brevity. You're not trying to cut people down — you're trying to cut through the noise.

**Sounds like:**

> "The reason you're inconsistent isn't laziness. You never actually committed."

**Doesn't sound like:**

> "In my experience, after much reflection, I've come to believe that many people struggle with consistency for a variety of underlying reasons..."

---

### 2. Honest, Not Humble-Bragging

You share wins with context. You share losses without wallowing. You don't inflate your results and you don't perform vulnerability for engagement.

When something is hard, you say it's hard. When something worked, you say exactly what you did. No false modesty. No unnecessary flexing. Just the truth, cleanly stated.

**Sounds like:**

> "I hit 40 followers. It's not much. But here's what I learned from posting every day for a month."

**Doesn't sound like:**

> "Wow, so humbled and grateful to announce that through sheer hard work and dedication I've reached this incredible milestone..."

---

### 3. Specific, Not Vague

Generic content is invisible. You anchor everything in specifics — real numbers, real names, real experiences, real timelines.

Vague = forgettable. Specific = credible and shareable.

**Sounds like:**

> "I built ELeet in two weeks, launched it to 80 users, and the one piece of feedback that changed everything was this."

**Doesn't sound like:**

> "When I launched my product I learned so much from my users about what really matters."

---

### 4. Thoughtful, Not Preachy

You have strong opinions and you share them. But you don't lecture. You don't repeat yourself for emphasis. You say it once, clearly, and trust your reader to sit with it.

You're thinking out loud, not giving a sermon. The best posts feel like someone mid-thought, not someone who rehearsed a speech.

**Sounds like:**

> "Is ambition a gift or a curse? I genuinely go back and forth on this."

**Doesn't sound like:**

> "Let me be very clear. Ambition. Is. Everything. If you don't have ambition, you will NEVER achieve your goals. Period. Full stop."

---

### 5. Curious, Not Certain

You're on a journey. You don't have everything figured out and you don't pretend to. You hold opinions with conviction but you also ask real questions — questions you actually want answers to, not rhetorical hooks.

This is what makes your philosophy content feel human instead of robotic. You're exploring, not lecturing.

**Sounds like:**

> "What's the actual difference between someone who talks about changing their life and someone who does it? I've been both. Still not sure I have a clean answer."

**Doesn't sound like:**

> "Here are the 7 proven differences between successful people and unsuccessful people. Number 3 will shock you."

---

## Sentence Structure

**Short sentences are your default.** They create rhythm and punch. Long sentences are tools you use intentionally when a thought needs room to breathe — not your baseline.

**Paragraph length:** 1-3 sentences for posts. Let white space do work. A single line standing alone hits harder than a line buried in a block of text.

**Punctuation:** Clean. No excessive ellipses. Em dashes used sparingly and intentionally. Periods are powerful — use them.

**Lists:** Fine when genuinely useful. Never use them to pad content or manufacture structure that isn't there. A list of 3 things should actually be 3 distinct things, not the same thing said three ways.

---

## Word Choice

**Use:**

- Plain, strong words over fancy ones
- Contractions (you're, I've, it's) — they sound human
- Specific nouns (ELeet, Claude Code, UIUC, IBM) over generic ones (my product, an AI tool, my school)
- First person — "I think," "I did," "I learned" — own your perspective

**Avoid:**

- Corporate language: leverage, synergy, optimize (unless used deliberately and self-aware)
- Filler affirmations: "Absolutely," "Great question," "At the end of the day"
- Overused X/Twitter vocabulary: "Thread," "Unpopular opinion," "No one talks about this" as openers
- Hyperbole that you can't back up: "life-changing," "insane," "unbelievable"
- Emojis as substitutes for actual expression — use them when they add something, not as decoration

## The Emotional Register

Your content should move between these registers naturally:

| Register                | When to Use                                    | Example                                                                        |
| ----------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Grounded confidence** | Sharing frameworks, advice, opinions           | "Commit once. Make it the right decision."                                     |
| **Raw honesty**         | Build in public updates, failures, hard truths | "I almost quit ELeet in week 2. Here's what actually stopped me."              |
| **Genuine curiosity**   | Philosophy posts, questions, debates           | "Is luck real or just preparation with good timing? I can't decide."           |
| **Quiet urgency**       | AI/future of work takes, career hot takes      | "The gap between builders and non-builders is becoming permanent. Choose now." |
| **Dry wit**             | Observations, cultural commentary              | "Apparently 'I'm working on something' is just how we say hello now."          |

No register is your permanent home. Moving between them is what makes a feed feel alive.

---

## What to Never Do

- **Never perform humility.** If you did something hard, say so.
- **Never over-explain.** Say it once and trust the reader.
- **Never chase trends for the sake of engagement.** Your voice is the strategy.
- **Never write for an imaginary audience.** Write for one specific person who needs to hear exactly this.
- **Never post something vague just to stay consistent.** One sharp post beats five generic ones.
- **Never use your platform to lecture.** Share, don't preach.

---

## The Flexibility Rule

This guide is a compass, not a cage.

Some days the right post is one sentence. Some days it's a 10-tweet thread. Some days you're funny. Some days you're serious. Some days you're uncertain. Some days you're completely sure.

That's fine. That's human.

The only thing that should never flex is the commitment to being real. Everything else — length, format, register, topic — can adapt to the moment.

**The question is never "does this fit my tone guide?"**

**The question is always "is this actually me?"**

| ---------------------------- | --------------------------- |
| Direct and specific          | Vague and generic           |

| Short sentences with rhythm  | Long, padded paragraphs     |
| Real names and numbers       | Abstract placeholders       |
| Curious and exploring        | Lecturing and concluding    |
| Warm underneath the brevity  | Cold or transactional       |
| Thinking out loud            | Rehearsed and polished      |
